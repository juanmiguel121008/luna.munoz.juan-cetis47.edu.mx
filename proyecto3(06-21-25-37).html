Hooke's Law — Connection between Physics and Mathematics

Explanation, formulas, interactive examples, and applications.

Introduction

Hooke's Law describes the linear elastic behavior of a spring: for small deformations, the restoring force is proportional to the displacement from the equilibrium position.

F = -k · x

Where:

  • F is the restoring force (N).
  • k is the spring or elastic constant (N/m).
  • x is the displacement from equilibrium (m). The negative sign indicates the force acts in the opposite direction of the displacement.

Physical Foundation

The law is valid as long as the material does not exceed its elastic limit. In that region, the behavior is linear and reversible. If exceeded, plastic deformations appear and the relationship no longer holds.

Relationship with simple harmonic motion (SHM): a block of mass m attached to a spring follows the equation

m·x¨ + k·x = 0

The solution is oscillatory with angular frequency ω = √(k/m) and period T = 2π·√(m/k).

Mathematical Connections

Hooke’s Law is a clear example of linear proportionality: F is a linear function of x with a slope of -k. Mathematically:

F(x) = -k·x

Related concepts:

  • Slope of the line: -k (in N/m).
  • Derivative: dF/dx = -k — indicates that the rate of change of force with respect to displacement is constant.
  • Integral: the elastic potential energy stored when stretching the spring from 0 to x is

U(x) = ∫ F(ξ) dξ = ½ · k · x²

Example: If k = 200 N/m and x = 0.05 m, then F = -200·0.05 = -10 N and the potential energy is U = 0.5·200·0.05² = 0.25 J.

Practical Applications

  • Vehicle suspension systems (in the elastic range of the spring).
  • Measuring instruments: spring dynamometers.
  • Modeling elastic links in engineering and computational physics.
  • Oscillators in laboratory experiments to study material properties.
